# Amazon Last-Mile ETA Prediction

## Project Overview

This project focuses on predicting the Estimated Time of Arrival (ETA) for last-mile deliveries using machine learning techniques. The goal is to improve delivery efficiency and customer satisfaction by providing accurate ETA predictions.

## Project Problem Statement

The main problem we aim to solve is to predict the ETA for last-mile deliveries based on various features such as delivery location, time of day, traffic conditions, and historical delivery data. Accurate ETA predictions can help optimize delivery routes, manage customer expectations, and improve overall logistics operations.

## Project Success Criteria

1. **Model Accuracy**: The ETA prediction model should achieve a high level of accuracy, ideally with a mean absolute error (MAE) of less than 10 minutes and a root mean squared error (RMSE) of less than 15 minutes.
2. **Scalability**: The model should be able to handle large volumes of delivery data and provide predictions in real-time.
3. **Integration**: The model should be easily integrable into existing delivery management systems.

## Evaluation Metrics

- MAE
- RMSE
- R2_score

## Dataset

The dataset used for this project is the Amazon Last-Mile Delivery dataset, which contains historical delivery data, including delivery locations, timestamps, traffic conditions, and other relevant features. The dataset is available in CSV format and can be accessed from the provided link [https://www.kaggle.com/datasets/sujalsuthar/amazon-delivery-dataset?resource=download]. The data consists of 43,739 rows and 16 columns, with the target variable being 'Delivery_Time' (in minutes).

## Current Progress

- **Data collection and loading**
  - Completed, and the dataset has been successfully loaded into a pandas DataFrame for further processing

- **Data cleaning**
  - Dropping the Order_ID column because it doesn't add any predictive value, rows with missing values in the 'Order_Time' column was dropped also and imputing missing values for 'Weather' and 'Agent_Rating' columns, after the conversion of date and time columns to appropriate formats.
  - After the data cleaning the shape of the data is now 43,648 rows and 15 columns.
  - The dataset is now ready for exploratory data analysis (EDA) and modeling.

- **Exploratory data analysis (EDA)**
  Insights have been drawn regarding the distribution of delivery times, the impact of various features on delivery times, and the relationships between different features.
  - The distribution of delivery times is normally distributed with a mean of around 124 minutes and a standard deviation of approximately 51 minutes.
  - Weather conditions, traffic conditions, and product categories have a significant impact on delivery times, weather like cloudy and foggy lead to longer delivery time, also, heavy traffic leads to longer delivery times. Product like groceries has the lowest delivery time among other product categories. While, Semi-urban area has the highest delivery time.
  - The correlation heatmap shows that there are no significant correlations between the numeric features and the target variable (Delivery_Time), which suggests that the relationships between the features and delivery time may be complex and non-linear, and may require more advanced modeling techniques to capture these relationships effectively.

- **Data splitting**
  The dataset has been split into training, testing, and validation sets using an 70-30 split for the training and testing sets, and a further 25% split of the training set for validation. The resulting shapes of the datasets are as follows:
  - Training set: (22914, 15)
  - Testing set: (13095, 15)
  - Validation set: (7639, 15)
  - The total data now consists of 43,648 rows and 15 columns.
  - The data was further split into features (X) and target variable (y) for each of the training, testing, and validation sets, preparing the data for modeling.
  - The data is now ready for baseline modeling and further feature engineering.

- **Feature engineering**
  - New features have been created based on the existing data, such as 'Distance_km' calculated using the haversine formula based on the latitude and longitude of the delivery location and the store location, and 'Delivery_Hour' extracted from the 'Order_Time' column to capture the time of day effect on delivery times. Other features such as 'Day_of_Week' and 'Is_weekend' were also created to capture the day of the week and whether the delivery is on a weekend, respectively.
  - Preparation time features was also created by calculating the time difference between 'Order_Time' and 'Pickup_Time' to capture the effect of preparation time on delivery times. After which the original 'Order_Time', 'Pickup_Time', and 'Order_Date' columns were dropped as they are no longer needed for modeling.
  - Then, the categorical columns were identified and one-hot encoded using the OneHotEncoder from scikit-learn, which allows for handling of unknown categories in the test and validation sets without causing errors.
  - The resulting datasets now have additional features which increase from 15 to 45. The features are ready for baseline modeling and further model development

  ### Baseline modeling
  - A baseline model was created using a simple linear regression algorithm to establish a benchmark for model performance. The model was trained on the training set and evaluated on the validation set, achieving an MAE of around 24.7 minutes and an RMSE of approximately 31.3 minutes, with an R2 score of around 0.63. This indicates that the baseline model is able to capture some of the variance in delivery times, but there is still room for improvement. The baseline model serves as a reference point for evaluating the performance of more complex models that will be developed in the next steps.

  - The mean delivery time in the training set is around 124 minutes, and the baseline model's MAE of 24.7 minutes indicates that, on average, the model's predictions are off by about 20% of the mean delivery time. This suggests a solid starting point for further model development, and there is potential to improve the model's performance by exploring more advanced algorithms, feature selection techniques, and hyperparameter tuning.

- **Error Analysis**
- An error analysis was conducted by examining the residuals (the differences between the actual delivery times and the predicted delivery times) from the baseline model. The distribution of residuals was visualized using a histogram with a kernel density estimate (KDE), which showed that the residuals are approximately normally distributed, indicating that the model's errors are random and not systematically biased. Additionally, a scatter plot of predicted values versus residuals was created, which revealed no clear patterns or trends, suggesting that the model's errors are homoscedastic (i.e., they have constant variance across all levels of predicted values). This error analysis provides insights into the performance of the baseline model and indicates that there is potential for improvement by exploring more complex models and feature engineering techniques.
